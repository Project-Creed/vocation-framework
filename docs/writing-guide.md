# Vocation Writing Guide

## Purpose

The Vocation Framework is a practical guide to useful work and durable contribution. It extends Ethosism into work, craft, livelihood, enterprise, professional trust, teams, leadership, succession, and legacy.

The book should help a reader answer: what work should I do, how should I do it, whom should it serve, and what kind of contribution should my labor leave behind?

## Core Commitments

Every chapter should connect back to five commitments:

1. Usefulness: produce work that serves real needs rather than only image, status, or private ambition.
2. Craft: build competence, standards, and care into the work itself.
3. Integrity: align promises, incentives, products, reputation, and behavior.
4. Stewardship: use talent, money, tools, teams, opportunity, and authority responsibly.
5. Contribution: leave people, institutions, and future workers better served because the work existed.

## Moral Method

Ground claims in five checks:

1. Objective reality: what does the work actually produce, cost, repair, improve, or damage?
2. Reciprocity: would the standard remain fair if you were the customer, worker, teammate, supplier, family member, apprentice, or future maintainer?
3. Integrity: does the work match the promises, values, quality claims, and reputation attached to it?
4. Stewardship: are talents, tools, money, time, and authority being used responsibly?
5. Long-term responsibility: what does this work pattern become across years, decades, or generations?

Do not treat work as mere status, income, identity, self-expression, or output. The standard is useful contribution that can be defended under reality, reciprocity, integrity, and time.

## Theology And Politics Stance

The Vocation Framework is secular and non-theological. It should be usable by atheists, agnostics, and religious readers.

The book may discuss markets, labor, management, entrepreneurship, technology, and public contribution, but it should not become a partisan economic program. It may name real exploitation, vanity, distorted incentives, anti-work evasion, and professional failure, but it should reason from consequences, reciprocity, integrity, stewardship, and long-term contribution rather than party identity or culture-war signaling.

## Voice

Write in a direct essay style. The tone should be serious, practical, and morally clear without becoming theatrical, sermon-like, hustle-driven, corporate, or ideologically smug.

Prefer:

- Concrete claims.
- Plain language.
- Clear distinctions between work, career, craft, livelihood, enterprise, and vocation.
- Accountability over status.
- Usefulness over image.
- Nuance without evasion.

Avoid:

- Hustle culture.
- Generic career advice.
- Romanticizing burnout.
- Anti-work cynicism.
- Entrepreneurship mythology.
- Corporate motivational language.
- Academic throat-clearing.
- Overstated certainty where work conditions vary by body, class, obligation, market, and season.

## Chapter Pattern

Most chapters should follow this shape:

1. Define the work domain and why it matters.
2. Identify the common failure mode.
3. State the Vocation standard for that domain.
4. Show how the standard follows from usefulness, craft, integrity, stewardship, and contribution.
5. Address tradeoffs, edge cases, or predictable objections.
6. End with a concise behavioral expectation or decision standard.

Do not force this pattern when a chapter needs a different structure, but keep the chapter moving from principle to practical judgment.

## Practice Blocks

Domain chapters should leave the reader with a usable practice. Include a short practice section or equivalent closing application that covers:

1. Plain standard: what the chapter asks the reader to take seriously.
2. Reality test: what the work actually produces, costs, improves, or damages.
3. Usefulness test: who is served, and whether the service is real.
4. Craft test: what standard of quality, skill, or care the work requires.
5. Integrity test: where promises, incentives, and behavior diverge.
6. Stewardship test: what talent, tool, money, authority, or opportunity must be handled responsibly.
7. Long-term test: what this work pattern becomes across years, decades, or generations.
8. First practice: one concrete behavior the reader can change this week.

The practice should make the essay actionable without turning the book into a checklist for its own sake.

## Consistency Checks

Before finishing a chapter or revision, check:

- Does the chapter depend on theology or partisan identity? If so, rewrite it in secular, nonpartisan terms.
- Does it explain why the work standard is good, not merely assert it?
- Does it respect role reversal for customers, workers, families, maintainers, apprentices, and future people?
- Does it account for incentives and predictable consequences?
- Does it distinguish useful work from status, image, and busyness?
- Does it connect to craft, integrity, stewardship, and contribution?
- Does it avoid excusing exploitation, vanity, laziness, burnout, dishonesty, or short-term extraction?
- Does it sound like Project Creed prose: clear, sober, concrete, and adult?
